## Purpose

This playbook synthesizes proven sales, negotiation, marketing, and influence frameworks into **universal principles** that apply across all communication contexts, then shows how each principle is applied within specific communication intents.

**Key principle:** Frameworks are not siloed. A storytelling principle that works in marketing also works in negotiation and cold email. This playbook maps frameworks across all their applications.

---

## How to Use This Playbook

**When analyzing a NEW framework from a video/article:**
1. Identify the universal principle (Storytelling, Tactical Empathy, Internal Tension, etc.)
2. Check if it's already in Part 1 — if yes, add new insights to that section
3. Trace where this principle applies → add examples to each relevant Intent section in Part 2
4. Create cross-references: *"See Universal Framework: [X]"*

**When drafting ANY communication:**
1. Identify the communication intent (Cold Outreach, Negotiation, Objection Handling, etc.)
2. Go to that section in Part 2
3. Read the Intent-Specific rules for that type
4. Look at the "Universal Frameworks in This Intent" subsection
5. Reference those frameworks in Part 1 as you draft

**Never limit a framework to one section.** Trace its applications.

# PART 1: UNIVERSAL FRAMEWORKS

Universal principles that apply across multiple communication intents. When you encounter a new framework, map it here first, then distribute it through Part 2.

## Framework #1: Tactical Empathy (Voss)

**Definition:** Understanding the feelings and mindset of another *in the moment* — hearing what's behind those feelings to increase influence. Not sympathy (not agreeing with values). Not false warmth. **Emotional intelligence in service of understanding.**

**Core Mechanics:**
- Humans are driven by System 1 thinking (fast, emotional) more than System 2 (slow, logical)
- Emotions are not obstacles — they are the primary vehicle for influence
- Understanding someone's emotional state gives you leverage to guide their decisions
- Empathy ≠ agreement. You can understand their position without accepting it.

**Key Techniques:**
- **Mirroring:** Repeat last 1–3 words in questioning tone → 4+ seconds silence → they elaborate
- **Labeling:** *"It seems like..."* / *"It sounds like..."* → silence → moves them from amygdala to rational brain
- **Accusation Audit:** List what they might think negatively about you; say it first (defuses objections before they arise)
- **Minimal Encouragers:** *"Yes,"* *"OK,"* *"I see"* — keep them talking without inserting your agenda

**Why It Works:**
- Mirroring: We're biologically drawn to similarity
- Labeling: Names emotions, moves them from fear to reasoning
- AA: Expectations reset; you seem understanding before they attack
- Silence: Gives them processing space; creates emotional openness

**Where It Applies:**
- Cold Outreach (build initial rapport)
- Negotiation (understand constraints, emotions, real objections)
- Objection Handling (find root emotion, not just stated objection)
- Relationship Building (demonstrate you understand them personally)
- Marketing (empathy in positioning yourself as guide, not hero)

## Framework #2: Internal Tension (Miner/NEPQ)

**Definition:** Creating emotional distance between their current state and desired state, such that they emotionally feel the need to close the gap. Not external pressure (your pitch). **Internal motivation (their realization).**

**Core Mechanics:**
- People reject external pressure (fight-or-flight response)
- People embrace conclusions they reach themselves (they fight for them)
- The salesperson's job is to ask questions that build awareness of the gap
- Gap awareness = internal tension = intrinsic motivation to solve

**The GAP Model:**
- **Current State:** Where they are now (problems, dissatisfaction, status quo)
- **Objective State:** Where they want to be (vision, success, solved state)
- **GAP:** The emotional distance between now and desired future
- **Your Job:** Build the GAP through skilled questioning

**Key Techniques:**
- **Problem Awareness Questions:** *"Do you like what you're...?"* / *"What would you change if you could?"* / *"Why is that important?"*
- **Two Truths Technique:** *"To me it sounds like things are going 100% perfect. What would you change?"* → Forces them to surface dissatisfaction
- **Consequence & Impact Questions:** *"So if this continues, what's going to happen?"* / *"How long can you sustain this?"* / *"What's the cost?"*
- **Verbal Pauses:** Slow down, use "..." → shows respect, gives processing time, builds emotional openness

**Why It Works:**
- External pressure triggers rejection
- Internal tension triggers self-persuasion
- When prospects come to their own conclusions, they fight for them

**Where It Applies:**
- Cold Outreach (discovery calls build gap awareness early)
- Negotiation (help them realize what's at stake)
- Objection Handling (real objection often hides a gap they haven't named)
- Marketing (help customers see the gap between current and desired)

## Framework #3: Storytelling (Miller/StoryBrand)

**Definition:** Using universal story structure to clarify your message and position the customer as the hero. Stories are how humans process information and make decisions — more effective than features, benefits, or logical arguments.

**Core Structure (SB7 Framework):**
> *A CHARACTER who wants something encounters a PROBLEM before they can get it. At the peak of their despair, a GUIDE steps into their lives, gives them a PLAN, and CALLS THEM TO ACTION. That action helps them avoid FAILURE and ends in a SUCCESS.*

**Key Elements:**
1. **Character:** The customer (what do they want?)
2. **Problem:** External + Internal + Philosophical (3 levels)
   - External: tangible, surface problem
   - Internal: emotional frustration
   - Philosophical: deeper "why it matters"
3. **Guide:** Your brand (empathy + authority, never the hero)
4. **Plan:** Process or agreement that reduces risk
5. **Call to Action:** Direct (buy) + Transitional (free value)
6. **Avoid Failure:** Cost of inaction
7. **End in Success:** Transformed life

**The Villain:** Root cause of problems (real, relatable, singular, not vague)

**Critical Rule:** Customer is hero. Brand is guide. Never position yourself as the hero.

**Why It Works:**
- Stories bypass logical resistance; they speak to System 1 thinking
- If you confuse, you lose — clarity is competitive advantage
- Customers don't care about your story; they care about their own
- Positioning as guide (not hero) creates trust and credibility

**Where It Applies:**
- Cold Outreach (narrative in email hook builds curiosity)
- Negotiation (story of why deal makes sense; story of what's at stake)
- Marketing (SB7 framework is the backbone of all messaging)
- Relationship Building (stories build trust over time)
- Content Creation (all educational content should follow story arc)

## Framework #4: Behavioral Change Stairway Model (Voss)

**Definition:** The sequence of stages that MUST happen in order to move someone toward agreement or action. You cannot skip stages. Each stage unlocks the next.

**The Five Stages (Sequential — No Skipping):**
1. **Active Listening** — genuine attention to what they're saying (not waiting to talk)
2. **Empathy** — demonstrating understanding of their perspective (Labeling, Mirroring)
3. **Rapport** — trust emerges from empathy (they feel safe)
4. **Influence** — only now can you guide their thinking (they're open to your ideas)
5. **Behavioral Change** — they take the action you suggest (and own it)

**Critical Point:** If you try to influence before rapport, you fail. If you try to persuade without empathy, you trigger resistance.

**Why It Works:**
- Matches how human trust is built (emotionally, sequentially)
- Shortcuts create distrust and resistance
- Once you reach Influence stage, change feels like their idea, not yours

**Where It Applies:**
- Negotiation (every negotiation must go through all 5 stages)
- Cold Outreach (discovery call stages 1–3; move to stage 4 if they're ready)
- Objection Handling (finding real objection requires stages 1–3 first)
- Relationship Building (deepening relationships = moving through stairway)

## Framework #5: Leverage (Voss)

**Definition:** Your ability to influence a negotiation through different forms of power. Three types exist. Strategic use of leverage creates movement without aggression.

**Three Types of Leverage:**

1. **Positive Leverage** 
   - Your ability to provide or withhold what they want
   - *"I have what you need"*
   - Use strategically, never as threat
   - Example: You have a property they want; they can't buy without financing from you

2. **Negative Leverage** 
   - Your ability to make them suffer or face consequences
   - *"If you don't act, this happens..."*
   - Never shove it down their throat — label it subtly instead
   - Example: *"I understand you're concerned about timing. Without a decision by Friday, this inventory sells to another buyer."*
   - ⚠️ Use rarely and carefully; erodes trust if overused

3. **Normative Leverage** 
   - Using their own norms/standards against them
   - Showing inconsistency between stated values and actions
   - Example: *"You said family is your priority, but this deal structure puts family assets at risk. How does that align?"*

**Why It Works:**
- Leverage guides without ultimatums
- Each type creates different kinds of pressure
- Understanding leverage dynamics helps you navigate negotiations without being aggressive

**Where It Applies:**
- Negotiation (primary framework for deal dynamics)
- Objection Handling (understanding what leverage the objection represents)
- Cold Outreach (subtle positive leverage: *"I only work with 10 clients; spots fill quickly"*)
- Relationship Building (normative leverage: *"You said you wanted to be a market leader; this positions you there"*)

## Framework #6: Rule of Three (Voss)

**Definition:** Get the same commitment three times in one conversation, in different formats. This ensures commitment is real, not just politeness.

**The Three Commitments:**
1. **Verbal acknowledgment:** *"So you're saying..."* (they state the commitment)
2. **Summarized confirmation:** Recap all terms in their language (they confirm with "That's right")
3. **Implementation question:** *"How will we..."* (they articulate the execution)

**Why It Works:**
- First "yes" may be politeness
- Second confirmation in their own words = genuine understanding
- Third (implementation) = they own it and have thought through execution
- If they hesitate at any stage, more work needed

**Where It Applies:**
- Negotiation (lock deals; ensure real commitment)
- Cold Outreach (Rule of Three on discovery call = qualified lead)
- Objection Handling (confirm real agreement before moving on)

## Framework #7: The Ackerman Model (Voss/Price Negotiation)

**Definition:** A disciplined framework for price negotiations that uses psychology and strategic anchoring to reach agreement.

**Six Steps:**
1. Set your **target price** (actual goal)
2. Open at **65% of target** (extreme anchor — resets their frame)
3. Plan three raises at **decreasing increments**: to 85% → 95% → 100%
4. Use **empathy and different ways of saying "No"** between raises to force them to counter
5. Final number: use **precise, non-round number** ($37,893 not $38,000)
6. At final number, **add small non-monetary item** (signals you've given everything)

**Why It Works:**
- Extreme anchor induces shock; resets their expectations
- Decreasing increments signal you're reaching limit
- Non-round final number feels researched, immovable
- Small item triggers reciprocity

**Where It Applies:**
- Negotiation (primary for price discussions)
- Deal discussions (when capital terms are negotiated)

## Framework #8: Negotiator Type Recognition (Voss/Black Swan)

**Definition:** Understanding the other person's communication and decision-making style so you can adapt your approach. Three types exist. 66% chance your counterpart is different from you.

**Three Types:**

1. **Analyst**
   - Methodical, data-focused, hates surprises
   - Slow to trust, needs time to process
   - Strategy: Use data, warn early, respect their process, give silence
   - Do NOT rush; do NOT outtalk

2. **Accommodator**
   - Relationship-first, warm, people-pleaser
   - May agree to things they can't deliver
   - Strategy: Build rapport, use "How" questions to pin down execution (they need clarity)
   - Great for initial connection; verify follow-through

3. **Assertive**
   - Time = money, results-focused, abrupt
   - Needs to be heard before they listen
   - Strategy: Use direct language, don't interrupt, don't outtalk
   - Give them space to articulate; then pivot

**Why It Works:**
- Everyone has a style; mismatched styles create friction
- Adapting to their style = faster rapport
- Understanding their type prevents miscommunication

**Where It Applies:**
- Negotiation (identify type early; adapt approach)
- Cold Outreach (adjust email/call tone to likely type)
- Objection Handling (what looks like objection may be communication style difference)

---

## Framework #9: "That's Right" vs. "You're Right" (Voss)

**Definition:** Two different responses with opposite meanings. "That's right" = genuine agreement + understanding. "You're right" = usually dismissal.

**The Difference:**

| Response | What It Means | Your Action |
|---|---|---|
| **"That's Right"** | Genuine understanding + empathy achieved. They own the conclusion. | Proceed. You've successfully moved through BCSM stages 1–3. Influence is now available. |
| **"You're Right"** | Likely dismissal or politeness. They want you to stop, not agreement. | Do NOT proceed. More empathy work needed. Use Mirroring/Labeling to go deeper. |

**How to Get "That's Right":**
- Summarize their position in THEIR language, not yours
- Cover all their points
- Tonality: emphasize parts that resonate with their values
- They should feel their position is understood, not countered

**Why It Works:**
- People own conclusions they feel understood
- "You're right" is a polite brush-off
- "That's right" is the turning point in negotiations

**Where It Applies:**
- Negotiation (primary marker of progress)
- Objection Handling (find real objection, not just "you're right" brush-off)
- Cold Outreach (on discovery call, push for "that's right")

## Intent #1: Cold Outreach & Discovery Calls

**Universal Frameworks in This Intent:**
- Tactical Empathy (build rapport early)
- Internal Tension (discovery call builds gap awareness)
- Storytelling (hook in email; narrative in call)
- BCSM (stages 1–3: listening → empathy → rapport)
- Negotiator Type Recognition (adapt to their style in call)
- "That's Right" (confirm understanding on call)

### Goal
Get a response → start a conversation → discover the GAP between their current state and desired state

### Tone
Friendly, direct, no pressure, value-first, curious

---

### Email Structure (Hook to Response)

**Subject Line:**
- A/B test problem-focused vs. curiosity-driven
- Problem-focused: *"Your [situation] is probably costing you $X"*
- Curiosity-driven: *"Quick question about your [situation]"*

**Body (Using Storytelling + Empathy):**
```
Opening (Personalization + Empathy):
"Hey [Name], I noticed you [specific thing about their company/situation]..."
↳ Shows you've done research; shows you understand their world

Hook (Problem or Curiosity):
"Most [their type of business] are [current problem/pain point]."
OR
"[Specific insight about their industry/situation]"
↳ Establishes relevance; builds internal tension (current state)

Pattern Interrupt (Transition + Value):
"The reason I'm reaching out is [why you can help]..."
↳ Moves from problem to solution trajectory

Soft Call to Action:
"Would it make sense to [minimal ask — call, chat, quick reply]?"
↳ Using Tactical Empathy: "Is now a bad time to talk?" > "Do you have 15 minutes?"

Signature:
Name, Title, Phone (brief, professional)
```

**Key Application of Frameworks:**
- **Storytelling:** Email tells story of current state → future possibility
- **Tactical Empathy:** Personalization shows understanding; minimal ask shows respect for their control
- **Internal Tension:** Problem statement builds awareness of gap

---

### Discovery Call Sequence (NEPQ 5-Stage Model + BCSM)

**Stage 1: Connection (5% of call)**
- Status frame: *"This call is pretty basic — it's really for us to find out what you're using now vs. what you're wanting, to see what that gap looks like."*
- Verbal pauses (slow down; respect their processing time)
- Stay neutral and detached — you're diagnosing, not selling
- Adapt tonality to their Negotiator Type (Analyst = calm/patient; Accommodator = warm/connected; Assertive = direct/efficient)

**BCSM Stage 1 (Active Listening):** Your sole focus is what they say, not your pitch.

---

**Stage 2: Engagement (85% of call) — The Core**

**Substage 2A: Situation (Build Current State)**
- "What are you using now?"
- "How long have you been doing [X]?"
- "Walk me through your process..."
- Ask 3–4 questions, then move on
- Use **Minimal Encouragers:** *"I see,"* *"OK"* — keep them talking

**Application of Frameworks:**
- **BCSM Stage 1 (Active Listening):** You're genuinely curious, not waiting to pitch
- **Tactical Empathy:** Listening without judgment builds safety

---

**Substage 2B: Problem Awareness (Build Internal Tension)**
- "Do you like what you're [doing/using]?"
- "What would you change about your [situation] if you could?"
- "Why is that important to you now?"
- "Has that had an impact on you?"
- Use **Two Truths Technique:** *"To me it sounds like things are going 100% perfect. What would you change if you could?"* (Nobody accepts 100% — forces them to surface pain)

**Application of Frameworks:**
- **Internal Tension:** You're not telling them they have a problem; they're discovering it
- **BCSM Stage 2 (Empathy):** Labeling emotional responses: *"It sounds like that's been frustrating..."*

---

**Substage 2C: Consequence & Impact (Deepen Gap Awareness)**
- "So, if this continues, what's going to happen?"
- "How long can you sustain this?"
- "What's the cost to you of [problem] every month/year?"
- "Who else in your organization is affected by this?"
- **Build emotional urgency** — this is where the GAP becomes real to them

**Application of Frameworks:**
- **Internal Tension:** Gap grows emotionally; they feel the need to close it
- **Tactical Empathy:** As they describe impact, Label their emotions: *"That sounds stressful..."*

---

**Substage 2D: Awareness & Buy-In (Confirm Gap Ownership)**
- "So, on a scale of 1–10, how important is it to you to [solve this problem]?"
- "What would solving this mean for you?"
- "If I could show you a way to [solve X], would it be worth a conversation?"
- **Push for "That's Right"** (Voss) — they own the gap, not you

**Application of Frameworks:**
- **"That's Right":** Summarize gap back to them; they should say "that's right" (not "you're right")
- **BCSM Stage 3 (Rapport):** If they own the gap, trust is building
- **Rule of Three:** First agreement (there's a gap) → confirm understanding → implementation question

---

**Stage 3: Transition (1% of call)**
- Bridge from discovery to next step: *"Based on what you've shared, I think we might be able to help. Would it make sense to [specific next step]?"*

**Application of Frameworks:**
- **Storytelling:** Next step is part of their journey (not your pitch)

---

**Stage 4: Presentation (10% of call)**
- Share your solution **only after** gap is acknowledged
- Position as one option among possibilities (never the only solution)
- Stay brief — they already know why they need this

---

**Stage 5: Commitment (5% of call)**
- Ask "How" questions to guarantee execution: *"How will we know we're on track?"* / *"How will we address it if we get off track?"*
- Get them to articulate next step in their own words (ownership)

**Application of Frameworks:**
- **Rule of Three:** Three commitments in different formats (verbal → summary → implementation)
- **BCSM Stage 5 (Behavioral Change):** They own the next action

---

### 5-Touch Email Sequence (Cold Campaign)

**Email 1 — Day 0 (Hook + Story)**
- Subject: Problem-focused or curiosity-driven (A/B test both)
- Body: Personalized story opening + value statement + soft ask
- Framework: Storytelling (current state), Tactical Empathy (personalization)
- No pitch; just curiosity

**Email 2 — Day 2 (Empathy + Accusation Audit)**
- Subject: Reference Email 1 lightly
- Body: Acknowledge potential objections (Voss Accusation Audit). Show you understand their world. Provide a small insight or data point
- Example: *"I know you're probably flooded with outreach. I'm reaching out because..."*
- Framework: Tactical Empathy (AA), Storytelling (why this matters)

**Email 3 — Day 5 (Social Proof / Authority)**
- Subject: New angle or case example
- Body: Brief case study or client story that mirrors their situation (Storytelling)
- No pressure; just proof you've solved this before
- Framework: Storytelling (hero's journey)

**Email 4 — Day 10 (Urgency / Gap Highlighting)**
- Subject: Problem escalation or window closing
- Body: *"You mentioned [situation] — here's why this matters now"* or *"This opportunity is time-sensitive..."*
- Build internal tension (Miner) around time/cost of delay
- Framework: Internal Tension, Leverage (time-limited)

**Email 5 — Day 14 (Final Ask + Off-Ramp)**
- Subject: *"Have you decided to solve [problem]?"* (Voss's "No"-oriented email — triggers loss aversion)
- Body: Final ask. If no response → graceful exit. *"I'll assume this isn't the right fit. If something changes, reach out."*
- Framework: Leverage (positive; implying scarcity)

---

### Key Tactics Deployed

- **Mirroring (Voss):** Repeat their last 1–3 words in a questioning tone; silence for 4+ seconds. They will elaborate.
- **Labeling (Voss):** *"It seems like..."* / *"It sounds like..."* → silence. Moves them from amygdala to rational brain.
- **Verbal Pauses:** Slow down. Strategic pauses = shows respect, gives them thinking room, builds emotional openness.
- **Calibrated Questions:** Use *"What"* and *"How"* only. Removes aggression. *"How would you..."* instead of *"You should..."*
- **Accusation Audit (Voss):** Before a call, list what they might think negatively about you/your company. Address it first in your message.
- **Non-Round Numbers:** When giving specifics, use precise numbers, not round ones. *"$47,350"* feels more researched and final than *"$50,000."*

## Intent #2: Negotiation & Deal Discussions

**Universal Frameworks in This Intent:**
- Tactical Empathy (understand constraints, emotions, real objections)
- BCSM (must go through all 5 stages; cannot skip)
- Internal Tension (help them realize what's at stake)
- Storytelling (story of why deal makes sense; story of what's at risk)
- Leverage (positive, negative, normative)
- Rule of Three (lock commitment)
- Ackerman Model (price negotiations)
- Negotiator Type Recognition (adapt approach)
- "That's Right" vs. "You're Right" (marker of progress)

### Goal
Move toward agreement on terms / close a deal / secure a commitment

### Tone
Collaborative, fair, methodical, understanding

---

### Pre-Negotiation Preparation

**Step 1: Identify Negotiator Type (Negotiate Your Approach)**
- **Assertive:** Control-oriented, abrupt, needs space to talk. Don't interrupt. Use direct language.
- **Accommodator:** Relationship-focused, warm, may overpromise. Build rapport. Use "How" questions to pin down commitment.
- **Analyst:** Data-focused, slow to trust, hates surprises. Provide information early. Give them silence to process.

**Step 2: Cold Read (Tactical Empathy)**
Imagine how your counterpart would describe this deal to a friend. What's their fear? Their timeline? Their real constraint?

**Step 3: Build an Accusation Audit (AA — Voss)**
List every negative thing they might be thinking about you, your offer, or your company. Prepare Labels to address these upfront:
- *"You're probably thinking I'm overpaying..."*
- *"I know this timeline feels impossible..."*
- *"I'm aware this deal hasn't gone smoothly..."*

Say these first. It defuses hidden objections before they become leverage against you.

**Step 4: Prepare Labels & Calibrated Questions**
- 5–10 labels ready to deploy based on their emotional state
- 5–10 calibrated questions to draw out information and build agreement
- Do NOT write a pitch — stay flexible and emotionally responsive

---

### The Negotiation Sequence

**Opening: Deliver the Accusation Audit (BCSM Stage 1 — Active Listening)**

Start with the most uncomfortable truth. Example:
> *"I know you're concerned that we don't have track record with this type of deal, and that's a fair point. It's something we're aware of and want to address head-on."*

Why:
- Defies expectations
- Demonstrates understanding (Tactical Empathy)
- Paves the way for agreement
- Removes their ammunition

---

**Building Rapport & Understanding (BCSM Stages 2–3: Empathy → Rapport)**

**Use Mirroring (Voss):**
- Repeat their last 1–3 critical words in a questioning tone
- Silence for 4+ seconds
- They will either elaborate or correct — either way, you've moved them from amygdala to rational brain

**Use Labeling (Voss):**
- *"It seems like the timeline is the biggest concern..."*
- *"It sounds like you're worried about [X]..."*
- Silence (4+ seconds)
- Watch them nod or correct — either reveals truth

**Use Minimal Encouragers:**
- *"Yes,"* *"OK,"* *"I see"* — keep them talking
- Verbal pauses ("...") — let them think and fill silence

---

**Push for "That's Right" — Not "Yes" (Voss / BCSM Stage 3)**

Summarize their position accurately and concisely in their own language:
- Cover all their points (nothing missed)
- Use their words, not yours
- Tonality: Emphasize the parts that resonate most with their values
- Their only option should be to say "that's right"

| Response | What It Means | Your Action |
|---|---|---|
| **"That's right"** | Genuine understanding + empathy achieved. They own the conclusion. | Proceed to influence stage. You've unlocked it. |
| **"You're right"** | Likely dismissal or politeness. | Do NOT proceed. More empathy work needed. Return to Mirroring/Labeling. |
| **Long silence** | They're processing | Do NOT fill it — let them think |

---

**Extracting Information & Finding Black Swans (Voss)**

Black Swans = unknown unknowns on their side. Three types:
- **Positive:** Unrevealed desire, budget, timeline
- **Negative:** Hidden objection, competing offer, internal politics
- **Leverage:** Legal constraint, CEO preference, deal-breaker

**Use Calibrated Questions to probe:**
- *"What about this is the biggest concern for you?"*
- *"How would solving [X] change things for your organization?"*
- *"What am I missing here?"*
- *"Why is that important?"*
- **Greatest:** *"How am I supposed to do that?"* (signals "No" without rudeness; forces them to problem-solve with you)

Probe until you find at least one Black Swan. It changes everything.

---

**Moving Toward Agreement (BCSM Stage 4 — Influence)**

**Use Rule of Three (Voss):**
Get the same commitment three times in one conversation:
1. First agreement: *"So you're saying [commitment]?"*
2. Label/summary confirmation: *"So to summarize, you're committing to [X]..."*
3. Implementation question: *"How will we know we're on track?"* (Forces them to articulate execution)

If they say *"I'll try"* or *"You're right,"* use more "How" questions — they're not truly committed.

---

**Bargaining — The Ackerman Model (Price Negotiations)**

If the conversation turns to price, follow this discipline:

**Step 1: Set Your Target Price**
(Your actual goal, what you'd accept)

**Step 2: Open at 65% of Target**
(Extreme anchor — induces shock, resets their frame)

**Step 3: Plan Three Raises at Decreasing Increments**
- First raise: to 85% of target
- Second raise: to 95% of target
- Third raise: to 100% of target

Between each raise, use empathy and different ways of saying "No":
- *"I appreciate that, but I can't get there."*
- *"I understand your position, but [constraint]."*
- Force them to counter first (shows how much headroom they have)

**Step 4: Final Number = Precise, Non-Round**
$37,893 instead of $38,000. Feels calculated, researched, immovable.

**Step 5: Add a Small, Non-Monetary Item**
After your final number, throw in something small they probably don't want (signals you've given everything, triggers reciprocity).

---

**Closing & Implementation (BCSM Stage 5 — Behavioral Change)**

**Ask "How" Questions to Lock Commitment:**
- *"How will we execute the first phase?"*
- *"How will we address it if we get off track?"*
- *"How will you present this to your team?"*

Their answers reveal real commitment. If they hedge, more empathy work is needed.

**Summarize Until "That's Right":**
- Recap all terms in their language
- They should feel they made the decision, not you
- Never declare victory — keep relationship intact for future deals

---

### Key Tactics

- **Strategic Umbrage (when needed):** Channel genuine anger at the proposal, not the person. *"I don't see how that would ever work."* Must be genuine — fake anger backfires.
- **Pronoun Analysis:** People using more third-person pronouns, complex sentences, and more words = likely less truthful or less important. More "I/me/my" = less powerful/important (understand their position in hierarchy).
- **Fair Language:** Use proactively at the start: *"I want you to feel you're being treated fairly at all times. Please stop me if you feel I'm being unfair and we'll address it."*
- **Normative Leverage (Voss):** Use their own norms/standards against them. *"You said family is priority. This structure puts family assets at risk. How does that align?"*

## Intent #3: Objection Handling & Pushback

**Universal Frameworks in This Intent:**
- Tactical Empathy (find the emotional root of objection)
- Internal Tension (the real objection often hides an unacknowledged gap)
- BCSM (must return to empathy stage if objection arises)
- "That's Right" (confirm you understand the real objection)
- Storytelling (reframe narrative of the problem)

### Goal
Understand the real objection / reframe without pressure / move toward agreement

### Tone
Curious, empathetic, non-defensive

---

### The Real Problem

Most stated objections are not about the objection stated. They're about:
- Unmet needs (they haven't revealed all their concerns)
- Emotional misalignment (they don't feel understood)
- Missing information (they don't have enough to commit)
- Hidden Black Swan (constraint or priority you haven't uncovered)

**Your job:** Find the real objection, not refute the stated one.

---

### Objection Handling Sequence

**Step 1: Pause & Don't Defend (BCSM Stage 1 — Reset to Listening)**
When you hear an objection:
- Do NOT immediately counter
- Do NOT explain why they're wrong
- Pause. Take a breath. Signal you're listening.
- This resets from "defend" to "understand" mode

---

**Step 2: Label the Objection (Tactical Empathy)**
Use Voss's Labeling technique:
- *"It seems like price is the biggest concern..."*
- *"It sounds like you're hesitant because [reason]..."*
- Silence (4+ seconds)

They will either agree (and elaborate) or correct you (revealing the real objection). Either way, you're now understanding, not defending.

---

**Step 3: Ask Calibrated Questions (Internal Tension + Empathy)**
- *"What about this doesn't work for you?"*
- *"What would need to be true for this to make sense?"*
- *"If we solved [objection], would [next step] make sense?"*
- *"Help me understand — what's the real blocker here?"*

Do NOT use "Why" — too aggressive. Use "What" and "How."

---

**Step 4: Reframe, Don't Argue (Storytelling + Tactical Empathy)**
Once you understand the real concern:
- Acknowledge it (don't dismiss)
- Show you've thought about it
- Present an option that addresses the core concern
- Use their words and values, not your logic

Example:
- **Stated objection:** *"It's too expensive."*
- **Questions reveal real objection:** *"I'm not sure it will actually work for our situation."*
- **Reframe:** *"I understand — you're not just asking about price, you're asking if this solves your [specific problem]. Let me show you how it did for [similar case]..."*

This reframes the narrative from "cost" to "capability" (Storytelling).

---

**Step 5: Push for Commitment (Rule of Three + "That's Right")**
- Use Rule of Three (get commitment three times)
- Ask "How" implementation questions
- If still hesitant, you haven't found the real objection yet — return to Step 2

---

### Common Objections & Real Concerns

| Stated Objection | Real Concern (Usually) | Reframe Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| *"It's too expensive"* | Unclear ROI / risk of wasted money | Show specific examples of cost savings or revenue generated. Use data to demonstrate value. |
| *"I need to think about it"* | They don't feel understood or convinced | Label: *"It seems like something doesn't feel right..."* Probe deeper to find real concern. |
| *"We've tried something like this before and it didn't work"* | Fear of repeating past failures | *"I understand. What was different about that situation?"* Learn from failure; show how you'd do it differently. |
| *"I need to check with [person]"* | Either real constraint (good) or avoidance (bad) | *"Of course. What questions do you think [person] will have?"* Help them prepare the conversation. |
| *"Can you send me more information?"* | Rarely about info; usually buying time or avoiding confrontation | *"Happy to. To make sure I send the right stuff, what's the biggest question in your mind right now?"* Address the real concern first. |

---

### Key Tactics

- **Assume Good Intent:** They're not trying to be difficult — they have a real concern. Find it.
- **NEVER Argue:** The person who argues loses. The person who asks questions wins.
- **Curiosity Over Certainty:** Act like you genuinely want to understand, not like you already know the answer.
- **The "No"-Oriented Email (Voss):** If they've gone silent, send: *"Have you given up on this project?"* Loss aversion triggers response.

## Intent #4: Relationship Building & Follow-Up

**Universal Frameworks in This Intent:**
- Tactical Empathy (remember what they care about)
- Storytelling (stories build deeper trust)
- BCSM Stage 3 (maintain rapport over time)

### Goal
Maintain trust / position for future deals / show genuine interest

### Tone
Warm, authentic, low-pressure

---

### Core Principle
**Retaining a customer is 5–25x cheaper than winning a new one.** (Black Swan)

Every deal is an opportunity to build trust for the next one, not just close the current one.

---

### Follow-Up Email Template (Post-Deal or Post-Call)

**Timing:** Within 24 hours of call/deal close

**Body Structure:**
```
Acknowledgment:
"Thanks for taking the time to discuss [topic] with me yesterday."
↳ Shows you noticed their time investment

Specific Recap:
"I appreciated your perspective on [specific thing they said].
You made a great point about [detail]."
↳ Proves you were listening; proves it mattered to you

Value Add (Tactical Empathy):
"Given what you shared about [their situation], I thought of [resource/intro/insight].
[Insert brief value — article, intro, data point]"
↳ Shows you were thinking of them outside the deal context

Soft Next Step:
"I'd love to check in with you in [timeframe] to see how [project] is progressing.
Feel free to reach out sooner if anything comes up."
↳ Relationship is ongoing, not transactional

Signature:
```

**Key principle:** Never make follow-up transactional. Always include something that shows you were listening and thinking of them.

---

### Check-In Cadence (Building Relationship Over Time)

- **After a deal closes:** 1-week check-in (how's implementation going?)
- **Monthly:** Brief email or call with relevant insight / news
- **Quarterly:** Deeper conversation about broader goals, challenges, wins
- **Annually:** Relationship review + planning for next phase

---

### Reference Building (Supporting Tactical Empathy)

Keep a running file of:
- What they care about (values, goals, constraints)
- What they've told you about their team/business
- Wins they've had / challenges they're facing
- Personal details (spouse's name, kids, hobbies — only if volunteered)

Use this in every future conversation to demonstrate continuity and genuine interest.

---

### Storytelling in Relationship Building

Share stories that illustrate lessons or concepts. Stories build trust over time more than facts do.

## Intent #5: Marketing & Messaging

**Universal Frameworks in This Intent:**
- Storytelling (SB7 Framework — foundation of all messaging)
- Internal Tension (help customers see the gap)
- Tactical Empathy (empathy in positioning guide role)

### Goal
Attract customers / clarify value / position your brand

### Tone
Clear, empathetic, benefit-focused

---

### The StoryBrand 7-Element Framework (SB7)

Every marketing message should follow this universal story structure:

> *A CHARACTER who wants something encounters a PROBLEM before they can get it. At the peak of their despair, a GUIDE steps into their lives, gives them a PLAN, and CALLS THEM TO ACTION. That action helps them avoid FAILURE and ends in a SUCCESS.*

---

### Element 1: A Character (The Customer as Hero)

Define ONE primary desire related to survival/safety/success. Don't list multiple — focus.

**Survival desires:**
- Conserve financial resources
- Conserve time
- Build social networks
- Gain status
- Accumulate resources
- Be generous
- Find meaning

**Examples:**
- Financial advisor hero desire: *"A Plan for Your Retirement"*
- Real estate agent: *"The Home You've Dreamed About"*
- Marketing agency: *"Double My Revenue Without Doubling My Spend"*

---

### Element 2: Has a Problem (Three Levels)

Every purchase has three levels of problem — address all three:

| Level | What It Is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **External** | Tangible, surface problem | *"I need to save for retirement"* |
| **Internal** | Emotional frustration | *"I'm anxious about the future"* |
| **Philosophical** | Deeper "why it matters" | *"My family should be secure"* |

**The Villain** = root cause, not just a feeling. Make it:
- A real source (not vague)
- Relatable (customers recognize it immediately)
- Singular (one villain per message)
- Real (don't manufacture fear)

Example: Villain = *"Market uncertainty"* (not *"feeling scared"*)

**Key insight:** The internal problem is the greatest motivator. Most companies only address the external. You win by addressing all three.

---

### Element 3: Meets a Guide (Empathy + Authority)

Customers need to see TWO things before trusting you:

**Empathy:** *"I understand your pain"*
- *"We understand how it feels to..."*
- *"Nobody should have to experience..."*
- *"Like you, we're frustrated by..."*

**Authority:** *"I know what I'm doing"*
- **Testimonials:** Let others speak (start with 3)
- **Statistics:** Numbers that demonstrate impact
- **Awards/Recognition:** Visual proof of competence
- **Logos:** Known clients you've served (B2B especially)

**Critical rule: The customer is the hero. Your brand is the guide. Never position yourself as the hero.**

---

### Element 4: Who Gives a Plan (Reduce Risk)

Plans are stepping stones — they reduce perceived risk of taking action.

**Type 1: Process Plan** (eliminates confusion)
- 3–6 steps showing how to engage with you
- Pre-purchase, post-purchase, or combined
- Name it: *"The [Company Name] Process"* or *"Our 3-Step System"*

**Type 2: Agreement Plan** (eliminates specific fears)
- Explicit commitments you make to customers
- Addresses their specific fears about doing business with you
- Example: No-haggle pricing, satisfaction guarantee, 24-hr response time

---

### Element 5: Calls to Action (Two Types — Use Both)

**Direct CTA** (Buy now)
- Purchase, call, schedule demo
- High friction — requires commitment
- Use for customers ready to act

**Transitional CTA** (Lower friction)
- Newsletter signup, free download, webinar, audit, intro call
- Low friction — builds trust before asking for money
- Use for early-stage awareness

**Use BOTH in your marketing.** High friction CTAs alone won't capture undecided prospects.

---

### Element 6: Helps Avoid Failure (Show the Cost of Inaction)

What happens if they don't act?
- Financial cost
- Opportunity cost
- Emotional cost
- Relationship/status cost

State it clearly but not with fear-mongering:
- *"Without a plan, you'll likely..."*
- *"If this continues, the impact will be..."*
- *"The cost of delay is..."*

---

### Element 7: Ends in Success (The Transformed Life)

Paint the picture of life after they buy:
- *"You'll have..."*
- *"Your team will be able to..."*
- *"You'll finally be able to..."*

Make it tangible and specific. Not vague benefits — concrete transformed state.

---

### Messaging Audit Checklist

Before publishing any marketing message, check:

- [ ] **Clear character?** Customers immediately see themselves as the hero
- [ ] **Real problem?** All three levels addressed (external, internal, philosophical)
- [ ] **Guides identified?** Empathy + authority demonstrated
- [ ] **Plan provided?** Process or agreement plan visible
- [ ] **Clear CTAs?** Both direct and transitional options
- [ ] **Failure consequences?** Cost of inaction stated
- [ ] **Success outcome?** Transformed life painted clearly
- [ ] **No jargon?** If someone's confused, you've lost them

## Intent #6: Content Creation & Thought Leadership

**Universal Frameworks in This Intent:**
- Storytelling (arc + hero's journey)
- Internal Tension (show the gap; help readers realize it)
- Tactical Empathy (understand what your audience cares about)

### Goal
Establish credibility / attract audience / position as expert

### Tone
Educational, authentic, specific

---

### Content Architecture (Three Types — All Needed)

| Type | Purpose | Frequency | Format |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Educational** | Answer questions, teach concepts | 2–3x/week | Blog, email, video, thread |
| **Narrative** | Tell stories that illustrate principles | 1x/week | Case study, story, anecdote |
| **Offer/Promotional** | Position your service/product | 1x/week | Soft ask, limited-time offer |

**Golden ratio:** 70% Educational + Narrative / 30% Promotional

---

### How to Find Content Ideas

**From Calls/Conversations:**
- Questions prospects keep asking → answer in content
- Objections you keep hearing → address in content
- Wins your customers celebrate → feature in case studies

**From Your Frameworks:**
- Steps in your NEPQ sequence → explain each as blog posts
- Deal structures you use → break down in threads
- Frameworks you apply → teach them publicly

**From Videos/Articles (Steve's Framework Distribution):**
- Tool mentions → write *"[Tool] vs. Alternatives"* comparison
- Frameworks → apply to your business, share lessons learned
- Concepts → create how-to guide

---

### Educational Content Template

**Hook (First 2 sentences):**
- Problem statement or surprising fact
- Make reader think, *"This applies to me"*

**Promise (Next paragraph):**
- *"By the end of this, you'll understand [concept] and how to [apply it]"*

**Education (Body):**
- 3–5 key insights or steps
- Use examples, stories, or data to illustrate
- Keep language clear — no jargon

**Application (Near end):**
- *"Here's how to use this..."*
- *"Try this today..."*

**Soft CTA (End):**
- Newsletter signup, free resource, reply with a question
- Not a hard sell

---

### Narrative Content (Stories & Case Studies)

**Structure:**
1. **Situation:** Who was this person? What was their situation?
2. **Challenge:** What problem or opportunity did they face?
3. **Action:** What did they do? What did you help them with?
4. **Result:** Specific outcome (numbers if possible)
5. **Lesson:** What can others learn?

**Key:** Make the customer the hero, not you. You were the guide.

---

### Thought Leadership Positioning

- **Specificity beats generality:** *"How to close real estate wholesaling deals under 30 days"* > *"Real estate investing tips"*
- **Data + story:** Share numbers, but ground them in real examples
- **Contrarian takes:** *"Why traditional marketing is failing"* gets more engagement than *"Do good marketing"*
- **Actionable insights:** Readers should be able to apply what you teach today

   - New insights layer on top
   - If new framework contradicts, document the nuance (context matters)
   - Richer playbook = better playbook

6. **Update version history at bottom**
   - Note what was added
   - Source and date

**Rule:** Treat this playbook as a living, growing library of proven frameworks. Each video/article adds depth. Nothing is deleted — only expanded.
